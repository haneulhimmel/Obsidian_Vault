- Maxwell's Equations
	- $$Faraday's\ Law: \nabla \times \vec{E} = -\frac {\partial {\vec{{B}}}}{\partial {t}}$$
	- $$Ampere's\ Law: \nabla \times {\vec{H}} = \vec{J}\ + \frac {\partial \vec{D}}{\partial t}$$
	- $$Gauss's\ Law\ of\ Electric\ Field: \nabla \cdot {\vec{D}} = \rho V$$
	- $$Gauss's\ Law\ of\ Magnetic\ Field: \nabla \cdot {\vec{B}} = 0$$
	-  Simplifications (Process to zero out components of an equation to allow for easier solving)
		i. Static
		- $$\frac {\partial}{\partial t} \approx 0 \therefore \nabla  \times {\vec{E}} = 0 \rightarrow \vec{E} = -\nabla V$$
		ii. Quasistatic
		- $$\frac {\partial}{\partial t} \approx 0 \therefore \nabla \times {\vec{H}} = \vec{J}$$
- Magnetostatics
	- Applications: Motors, Magnetic Data Storage (Hard Drive), Cathode Ray Tube
- Electromagnetic
	- $\frac {\partial}{\partial t}$ is not negligible --> Under the free space assumption; hence the current density is 0 b/c no particles to conduct through
	- Applications: Antennas, Optics, Transmission Lines
	- Differential equations on the RHS are "forcing functions"
		i. $$\nabla \times {\vec{E}} = -\frac {\partial{\vec{B}}}{\partial{t}}$$
		ii. $\vec{H}$ represents the magnetic field and $\vec{D}$ the displacement $$\nabla \times {\vec{H}} = \frac {\partial{\vec{D}}}{\partial{t}}$$
		iii. Constitutive Relations $$\vec{B} = \mu \vec{H} \quad \& \quad \vec{D} = \epsilon \vec{E}$$
